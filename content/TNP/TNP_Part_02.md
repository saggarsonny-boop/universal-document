# 🩺✨ Part 2: The Identity I Thought Was Me
*The TNP Series | By Dr. Sonny Saggar | May 18, 2026*

For twenty-five years, I knew exactly who I was. 

If you asked me to introduce myself, the answer was automatic, practiced, and bulletproof. I was an ER physician. I was the founder of an urgent care network of four centers. I dabbled in real estate in depressed areas in an effort to resuscitate them, specifically North City and Downtown St. Louis. I was a provider who carried the weight of my family, my employees, and my patients without breaking a sweat. 

I was the guy who could walk into a Level 1 trauma, assess a chaotic, bleeding room in three seconds, and issue the orders that saved a life. I was the guy who could leave that shift, put on a suit, and negotiate a triple-net lease for a new commercial property. 

I was invincible because I was useful. 

But I didn’t realize that over time, I had stopped being a person. I had become a utility. My entire identity, my self-worth, my ego, my relationships, was inextricably linked to my output. I believed that as long as I was generating value, building fortresses of security for my family, and saving lives in the ER, I was untouchable. 

### The Armor We Wear

In medicine, we are trained to build armor. You cannot survive a decade in the emergency room if you let every tragedy pierce your chest. You learn to compartmentalize. You learn to suppress. You learn to control the room.

That armor is necessary for survival in the hospital. But the tragedy of the modern physician is that we forget how to take the armor off when we go home. 

I wore my armor everywhere. I wore it in my marriage. I wore it in the boardroom. I believed that vulnerability was a liability, and that asking for help was a failure of leadership. I thought I was protecting my wife and my children by shielding them from the brutal stress of my reality. I thought I was being the ultimate provider.

But the perception gap was massive. While I thought my relentless drive and unbreakable facade made me a pillar of strength, it actually made me an island. I was physically present, but emotionally impenetrable. 

### Stripping the Titles

What happens when the system turns on you? What happens when the things that define you are systematically stripped away?

When the legal fire started, the titles vanished. Suddenly, I wasn't the brilliant ER doctor. I wasn't the invincible founder. The hospital boards I sat on, the businesses I built, the social status I had accumulated, it all evaporated overnight. 

When you strip away the white coat, the stethoscope, the corner office, and the bank accounts, what is left? 

For a terrifying period of time, I looked in the mirror and didn't know the answer. The identity I thought was me was gone. I felt like I had ceased to exist. 

### The Autopsy of an Ego

Looking back, the collapse of my identity wasn't just a tragedy; it was a necessary autopsy. 

I had to lose the titles to realize that I was never the titles. The drive that took me from a kid in Southall, London, to an Oxford-educated physician wasn't dependent on a medical license or a real estate portfolio. The resilience that allowed me to outwork everyone in the room wasn't tied to an urgent care clinic. 

The identity I thought was me was a fragile construct. It was built on the validation of a broken system. 

When the system took it away, I thought I had lost everything. I didn't know it yet, but I was actually being given a blank slate. The fire had burned down the fortress, but the foundation remained. And for the first time in my life, I had to figure out how to build something that couldn't be taken away.
